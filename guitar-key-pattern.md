# Guitar Key Pattern

_Using the guitar to find notes & chords in a major or natural minor key._

## Review of major scale chords

Here are the chord types defined for the major scale.

| Root  |     2 |     3 |     4 |        5 |     6 |          7 |
|-------|-------|-------|-------|----------|-------|------------|
| major  | minor | minor | major | dominant | minor | diminished |

That is, the root note in a given major scale corresponds to a major chord, the second note corresponds to a minor chord, and so on.

Here are examples in the keys of C and F using only triad chords. Remember that the major scale from a root note is constructed using the formula Root-W-W-H-W-W-W-H.

| C | D  | E  | F | G  | A  | B    |
|---|----|----|---|----|----|------|
| C | Dm | Em | F | G  | Am | Bdim |

| F | G  | A  | B‚ô≠ | C | D  | E    |
|---|----|----|----|---|----|------|
| F | Gm | Am | B‚ô≠ | C | Dm | Edim |

## Finding the major scale chords on the guitar

Here is a general form for a major scale pattern on the low E and A strings, given a root note R.

| E | A |
|---|---|
| 6 | 2 |
| - | - |
| 7 | 3 |
| R | 4 |
| - | - |
| - | 5 |

Replacing the notes with the type of chord from the major scale, here are the chords corresponding to each note.

| E   | A |
|-----|---|
| m   | m |
| -   | - |
| dim | m |
| M   | M |
| -   | - |
| -   | M |

 - M = major
 - m = minor
 - dim = diminished

Observe that the major chords correspond to the notes associated with:

 - root note on the E string
 - the note below it on the A string
 - the note 1 whole step above the note on the A string

The minor chord notes follow the same pattern except starting with the note on the E string 3 half steps below the root note (the relative minor of the root note).

The diminished chord in the key is associated with the note a single half step below the root note on the E string.

## Natural minor scale chords

The natural minor scale for a root note R has the same notes and chords as R's relative major note's major scale, where the relative major of R is 3 half steps above R.

Recall the major scale for G (G A B C D E F‚ôØ):

| E  | A |
|----|---|
| E  | A |
| -  | - |
| F‚ôØ | B |
| G  | C |
| -  | - |
| -  | D |

G is the relative major of E / E is the relative minor of G. The notes in the E minor scale are the same as the G major scale except the root note starts with E instead of G: E F‚ôØ G A B C D.

Recall the chords associated with each note:

| E   | A |
|-----|---|
| m   | m |
| -   | - |
| dim | m |
| M   | M |
| -   | - |
| -   | M |

The chords for the E minor scale are the same as the chords for the G major scale. The major chords are G, C, and D. The minor chords are Emin, Amin, and Bmin. The diminished chord is F‚ôØdim.

This pattern works for any relative major/minor pair.

The pattern for the minor scale starts at a different root note even though it has the same notes:

| E | A |
|---|---|
| R | 4 |
| - | - |
| 2 | 5 |
| 3 | 6 |
| - | - |
| - | 7 |

From this, the minor scale formula is derived as Root-W-H-W-W-H-W-W.

And here are the chords for the minor scale based on the root note.

| Root  |          2 |     3 |     4 |     5 |     6 |        7 |
|-------|------------|-------|-------|-------|-------|----------|
| minor | diminished | major | minor | minor | major | dominant |

## Adding the 7th

- Each major chord maps to a major 7th chord
- The dominant chord maps to a 7th chord (dominant 7th)
- The minor chords map to minor 7th chords
- The diminished chord maps to a minor 7 flat 5 chord
